Philleese’s Manifesto

 

The Goal of Philleese's Photographs

The Goal is to capture – in a series of photographs – how it feels to have fun in the club.

  1. ★    To capture the dynamics, mood, and ambiance of us enjoying club nights, as accurately as possible

  2. ★    To capture people as they are, with a preference for genuine over posed expressions

  3. ★    To visually represent the feeling and mood we feel at that place and time

  4. ★    To make the viewer feel what it feels like to be there with us

  5. ★    To experiment and find fun, new, and interesting effects

A  “good photo” is one that communicates what it's like to be in the club to someone who wasn't there that night, or better yet, someone who's never been there.

The Continuum Between Snap Shots and Drunk Shots

The first step to attaining The Goal means recognizing the continuum between cold documentation and total incomprehensibility.

At one end of the scale is cold documentation, or snap shots:

  1. ★    A photo may capture exactly how a person looks at a given instant, yet fail to express how the person feels

  2. ★    Photos taken with short shutter speeds, and in particular when the main source of light is a flash, often fail to capture the dynamics, mood, and ambiance of the moment

  3. ★    Portraits are fine, but are better suited to portrait studios and mug books than clubs

At the other end of the scale is total incomprehensibility, or drunk shots:

  1. ★    Photos that are so abstract that no one knows what the subject is may indeed capture some interesting shapes, but likewise fail to capture and communicate the moment

  2. ★    These photos often communicate only that the photographer has had “one too many”

A photograph that expresses how it feels to be in the club, at that moment, of necessity must lie somewhere between the extremes of this continuum.

Subgoals

Capturing and expressing feelings in a two-dimensional stagnant image is challenging.  Identifying these Subgoals should help:

  1. ★    To capture people having fun and drinking

  2. ★    To capture people posing, socializing, and dancing

  3. ★    To capture people in their element, as they look today, here and now

  4. ★    To capture people in context – along with their clothes, their friends, and the club's lighting and decor

  5. ★    To capture people being buzzed – but not embarrassingly so

  6. ★    To capture people with their hair down – both literally and figuratively

  7. ★    To capture people in motion – not posing but not necessarily dancing either

  8. ‣    This requires capturing just the right amount of blur – enough to show movement but not so much that the image becomes incomprehensible

  9. ★    To capture people using their cell phones

  10. ‣    Today these are ubiquitous, but someday they will be obsolete

  11. ‣    The light of a cell phone shining in a person's face has the potential to yield a wonderfully artistic portrait of how the person looks in the club in real life

  12. ★    To capture people being themselves – and in some cases even being their "secret selves"

This list is just a starting point, and is not by any means comprehensive.

Specific Techniques, Processes, and Tools

Past experience has shown that these techniques can help achieve The Goal.

Lighting

After the subject matter, light is the second most crucial element in photography.

  1. ★    Using long exposure times – instead of or in addition to the flash – goes a long way towards helping to create an image that resides on a good place on the documentation-incomprehensibility continuum

  2. ‣    The length of the exposure and whether a flash will help or hurt, depends a great deal on what – if any – additional light is available

  3. ‣    When using long exposures with a flash, having the subject move abruptly and quickly after the flash fires can yield an interesting “ghostly” effect – which supports the goal because we are all here for only a very short time

  4. ★    Attaining The Goal requires not just acknowledging but also taking advantage of the club's lighting

  5. ‣    On the one hand, not having control over these lights can be problematic

  6. ‣    However, it is also possible to embrace this lack of control, and recognize these lights can yield very interesting “chance operations”

  7. ‣    Experimentation and familiarity with the lighting used in specific rooms of specific clubs is essential

  8. ★    A fun option is to use supplemental lights, especially cell phones and different-colored LEDs

  9. ‣    The light from cell phones' displays – when it shines in peoples' faces while texting – opens up a wonderful opportunity to capture people as they look in real life

  10. ‣    Experimentation has shown that keeping the LEDs out of the photo gives the lighting they add a wonderful sense of mystery

  11. ‣    Having one or more designated lighting people shine and even move one or more LEDs makes it easier to keep the light out of the frame

  12. ★    Another fun technique involves taking advantage of flashes from other cameras

  13. ‣    Photos of people taking photos is most certainly in keeping with The Goal

  14. ‣    Using a long exposure to capture the flash from another camera in addition to that of the primary camera can make for an interesting double image effect

  15. ‣    This is a great way to capture movement without the image being incomprehensible

Camera Orientation

One might wonder why camera makers create cameras that default to the landscape orientation, when these are so often used to take portraits.  Also, considering the ability to use tools such as photoshop, certainly a square format camera might be a wonderful idea.  Jus' sayin'....

  1. ★    Portraits, especially of individuals and very small groups, should be done in portrait mode

  2. ‣    This allows the image to include their garments, which in many cases is crucial, because fashion plays an important part of the club scene

  3. ★    It is often best to take portraits of groups of people in landscape mode

  4. ‣    In this case it is advisable to back up so that the photograph can include the garments

Camera Stabilization and Movement

To avoid incomprehensibility, and to capture the movement of people rather than movement of the camera, it is sometimes advisable to stabilize the camera when using long exposure times.

  1. ★    Sometimes a tripod can be very helpful, but it must be a small one, because a full-sized one would ruin the atmosphere

  2. ★    To do: purchase and experiment with a monopod

Moving the camera can also make for some interesting images.  This is most effective when using the flash, and in particular when there are ambient points of light, because the movement of the camera renders these points as lines.

Picking Highlights, the Best, and the Best of the Best

Perhaps more so than the other art forms, photography - and dancing, but that’s another story - suffer from the “anybody can do that” syndrome.  It’s a simple matter to click the shutter and post the results to the internet.

Identifying a Goal, acknowledging that the result is not completely under any one person’s control, and declaring a willingness to experiment makes identifying successes, and failures, imperative.  However all art is subjective to a certain extent, making these terms relative to the individual’s perceptions and prejudices - or lack thereof - at any given moment.

Contact Sheets: All but the Most Embarrassing

Individuality and continually evolving aesthetics make it important to post “contact sheets,” or pages that contain the vast majority of images taken over a given period of time.  This allows each person to decide for themself which images are “good” or “bad,” regardless of the extent to which the image conforms to The Goal.

Given that these photos are taken by drunk people of drunk people, it’s important that any images that may be considered embarrassing to the photographer or subject not be posted, and that the posted images appear in a secret, private place. 

And for the benefit of the community, it is likewise imperative that people trusted to view these contact sheets not copy potentially embarrassing images of any one other than themselves.

Highlights: Interesting, Silly, and Token Photos

The first step in helping the viewer understand the vision is to identify “highlights.”  Photos posted as highlights do not necessarily need to adhere to The Goal, and can be:

  1. ★    simply interesting or unconventional

  2. ★    of someone striking a silly pose

  3. ★    even just the “token” shot pulled from a series

Posting contact sheets makes it possible to select highlights without fear of hurting the feelings of any single subject or image.  Yes, images are by their very nature complex and can have feelings too!

The Best

Having identified interesting, unconventional, silly. and “token” shots, the next step is to identify images that represent progress toward achievement of The Goal. 

It is essential that these images appear in a public place, where people can voice their opinions of the images. This allows further identification of “good” versus “bad” images to take advantage of the Wisdom of the Crowd.

At this time it appears that the best place to post these photos is on Philleese’s facebook page.

The Best of the Best

The Best of the Best photos must:

  1. ★    Successfully achieve The Goal

  2. ★    Receive favorable comments and votes from The Crowd

  3. ★    Be severely limited in number, e.g. certainly to no more than 100 for a single year

At this time it appears that the best place to post these photos is on Philleese’s flickr page.

To contain costs, the number of these photos must be less than the limit imposed by a free flickr account, which means that at most 200 photos may be considered The Best of the Best for “All Time.”

Request for Blanket Permission to Take Candid Photos

Capturing candid rather than posed shots can go a long way towards achieving The Goal.  It would be helpful to obtain “blanket permission” from club regulars to take candid photos, rather than continue to ask them each time, only to have them pose and make the image less realistic.

Now that you’ve found this “secret” site, and know all about The Goal, do you give us your Blanket Permission to Take Candid Photos?

Look at the photos here.  Don’t the candid, non-posed shots give you a much better sense of what it feels like to be in the club?  Don’t the posed photos look cheesy in comparison?

Hopefully reading this lets you know what we’re trying to do, and also gives you something to think about!
